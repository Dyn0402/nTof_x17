\begin{abstract}
\noindent
We analyse the final det3 weekend campaign: a \SI{2.4}{h} run at the
operating point (resist \SI{490}{V}, drift \SI{1000}{V}; configured for
\SI{12}{h} but stopped early for the overnight p2 run), a six-point drift-HV
scan (\SIrange{100}{1100}{V}), and two resist-HV scans.  Four results:
(i)~the universally observed \emph{off-diagonal} micro-TPC angle correlation is
quantitatively explained as an additive charge-spreading bias,
\(\tan\thdet \simeq \tan\thref \pm w/z_{\mathrm{vis}}\) with
\(w/z_{\mathrm{vis}} = 0.115\) --- a detector constant, not a rotation and not
an analysis bug;
(ii)~exploiting this understanding, a bias-free \emph{ridge-fit} estimator
measures the electron drift velocity to \SIrange{1}{3}{\percent} statistical
precision per 15-minute scan point, giving
\(\vd(\SI{1000}{V}) = \SI{28.1 \pm 0.7}{\umns}\) and the full \(\vd(E)\) curve;
(iii)~the drift gap is mechanically \(\approx\SI{30}{mm}\), but only the
\SI{19.4}{mm} of drift column closest to the mesh produces recorded signal:
the strip arrival-time distribution carries an exponential tail beyond the
apparent edge that terminates at the \SI{30}{mm} drift time, and the strip
amplitude decays with drift \emph{depth} --- electrons from the far third of
the gap are lost to \textbf{attachment} in the contaminated gas;
(iv)~the measured \(\vd(E)\) is far below the Magboltz prediction for clean
Ar/iC\(_4\)H\(_{10}\) 95/5; with the \SI{30}{mm} field scale it is reproduced
by Ar/iso 95/5 with \SIrange{1}{1.5}{\percent} water, and the measured
attachment length matches \SIrange{0.2}{0.4}{\percent} O\(_2\) --- one
air/moisture contamination explains both anomalies.  A wrong bottle of
Ar/CO\(_2\) 90/10, though matching \(\vd(E)\) closely, is excluded by the
amplification (\(+\SI{50}{V}\) at iso-gain) and by its zero attachment.
\end{abstract}

\section{The micro-TPC measurement}
%=====================================================================
\label{sec:utpc}

\subsection{Principle}

A single conversion/drift gap of depth $g$ sits above the micromesh; the
avalanche charge is read out on strips of pitch $p=\SI{0.78}{mm}$
(Fig.~\ref{fig:principle}).  A muon crossing at angle $\theta$ (measured from
the detector normal, i.e.\ from the drift direction) leaves an ionisation
trail spanning the \emph{full} gap in $z$ and $g\tan\theta$ in $x$.  (In this
detector, as Sec.~\ref{sec:gap} shows, only the fraction of the column within
$z_{\mathrm{vis}}\approx\SI{19.4}{mm}$ of the mesh survives the drift with
enough charge to be recorded --- everywhere below, ``the gap'' as seen by the
strips means this \emph{visible column} $z_{\mathrm{vis}}$, not the
\SI{30}{mm} electrode spacing.)  Electrons
drift to the mesh at velocity $\vd$, so the strip at horizontal distance
$\Delta x$ from the track's mesh-crossing point receives its charge later by
\begin{equation}
  \Delta t \;=\; \frac{\Delta z}{\vd} \;=\; \frac{\Delta x}{\vd\tan\theta}.
  \label{eq:utpc}
\end{equation}
Fitting strip signal time versus strip position (amplitude-weighted straight
line, anchored at the earliest hit) yields a slope whose inverse is
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\mathrm{d}x}{\mathrm{d}t} \;=\; \vd\tan\theta
  \qquad\Longrightarrow\qquad
  \tan\thdet \;=\; \frac{1}{\vd}\,\frac{\mathrm{d}x}{\mathrm{d}t}.
  \label{eq:angle}
\end{equation}
Equation~\eqref{eq:angle} is the micro-TPC angle estimator: it needs the
drift velocity $\vd$ as an external input, and conversely, once an external
reference ($\thref$ from M3) is available, the \emph{correlation between
$\thdet$ and $\thref$ measures $\vd$}.  That is the core idea exploited
throughout this note.  In the analysis code the raw fit slope is
$s \equiv \mathrm{d}x/\mathrm{d}t$ in \si{\umns}; the detector-frame slope
vector $(s_x, s_y)$ is rotated by the alignment angle
($\theta_{\mathrm{align}} \approx \SI{90}{\degree}$) into the M3 frame before
any comparison, so det-X pairs with ref-Y and vice versa.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{diagram_principle.png}
\caption{Micro-TPC principle. Left: an inclined muon (red) ionises the gas
along the full \SI{30}{mm} drift gap; electrons (blue) drift down at $\vd$
and arrive at the mesh with delays proportional to their production height.
Charge from above $z_{\mathrm{vis}}\approx\SI{19.4}{mm}$ (pink band) is lost

\label{fig:principle}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Why the micro-TPC mode matters here}

Position resolution (Fig.~\ref{fig:residuals}) uses only the charge centroid
and is insensitive to everything discussed below.  The micro-TPC \emph{slope},
however, is the detector's unique selling point --- per-plane track angles
from a single thin detector --- and it is exactly this quantity that shows the
systematic effects analysed next.

%=====================================================================
\section{Why the angle correlation is always off-diagonal}
%=====================================================================
\label{sec:offdiag}

\subsection{The observation}

Figure~\ref{fig:anglecorr} shows the production angle-correlation plot for the
Saturday long run: reference angle $\thref$ (M3) versus detector angle
$\thdet$ (Eq.~\ref{eq:angle}, with $\vd$ set by the production
diagonal-projection scan).  The correlation is strong --- the micro-TPC works
--- but three features stand out, and they appear \emph{identically in every
detector of the June campaign} (det2 shown for comparison in
Fig.~\ref{fig:det2corr}):

\begin{enumerate}
  \item the dense ridge runs \emph{parallel} to the $y=x$ diagonal but is
        displaced \emph{outward}: $|\thdet| > |\thref|$ by
        $\sim$\SIrange{5}{6}{\degree};
  \item there are essentially \emph{no} events with
        $|\thdet| \lesssim \SI{5}{\degree}$ --- an exclusion gap around the
        vertical;
  \item events with $\thref \approx 0$ form \emph{horizontal arms} extending
        to large $|\thdet|$ of either sign.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Why it is \emph{not} a rotation or misalignment}

A residual in-plane rotation is absorbed by the alignment (the rotation scan
converges to \SI{0.05}{\degree} steps and the slope vector is rotated
accordingly).  More fundamentally, \emph{any} rotation or constant angular
offset would shift the correlation in \emph{one} direction --- up or down ---
whereas the observed displacement \textbf{flips sign with the track
direction}: tracks leaning one way reconstruct too steep in that direction,
tracks leaning the other way too steep in the other.  The offset is odd in
$\theta$, i.e.\ it behaves like $\;\mathrm{sign}(\theta)\cdot\mathrm{const}$,
which no rigid-body transformation produces.  An out-of-plane tilt of the
detector would also be a one-directional shift, and is excluded for the same
reason.

\subsection{The mechanism: fixed time span, inflated cluster width}
\label{sec:mechanism}

The key geometric fact (Fig.~\ref{fig:offsetdiagram}): \textbf{every cosmic
crosses the full drift gap}, so the recorded cluster always spans the entire
\emph{visible} column depth $z_{\mathrm{vis}}$ in $z$ (the fixed depth at
which drifting charge drops below threshold; Sec.~\ref{sec:gap}).
Consequently the \emph{time} extent of every cluster is the same,
\begin{equation}
  \Delta t_{\mathrm{cluster}} \;\approx\; \Tsat = z_{\mathrm{vis}}/\vd,
  \qquad \text{independent of } \theta,
\end{equation}
while the \emph{spatial} extent is
\begin{equation}
  \Delta x_{\mathrm{cluster}} \;\approx\;
  \underbrace{z_{\mathrm{vis}}\tan\theta}_{\text{geometric}}
  \;+\; \underbrace{w}_{\text{non-geometric}},
\end{equation}
where $w$ collects everything that widens the charge footprint on the strips
without carrying drift-time information: charge spreading across the
resistive layer, transverse diffusion, capacitive coupling to neighbours, and
$\delta$-electrons.  The fitted slope is then, schematically (writing
$z_{\mathrm{v}} \equiv z_{\mathrm{vis}}$),
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\mathrm{d}x}{\mathrm{d}t}
    \;\approx\; \frac{z_{\mathrm{v}}\tan\theta + w\,\mathrm{sign}(\theta)}{z_{\mathrm{v}}/\vd}
    \;=\; \vd\left(\tan\theta \pm \frac{w}{z_{\mathrm{v}}}\right)
  \qquad\Longrightarrow\qquad
  \boxed{\;\tan\thdet \;\approx\; \tan\thref \pm \frac{w}{z_{\mathrm{vis}}}\;}
  \label{eq:offset}
\end{equation}
an \textbf{additive, angle-independent excess in tan-space} whose sign
follows the track sign.  (The sign attaches to $w$ because the fit interprets
the extra width as extra track length in the direction the track already
leans; for a perfectly vertical track the sign is random --- hence the
horizontal arms.)

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{diagram_offset.png}
\caption{The off-diagonal mechanism. Left: a vertical track --- the time span
still covers the full visible column ($z_{\mathrm{vis}}/\vd$), but the
cluster width is pure spreading, $w\approx\SI{2}{mm}$; the fit converts $w$
into a fake angle $\arctan(w/z_{\mathrm{vis}})\approx\SI{6.5}{\degree}$ of
random sign. Right: an inclined track --- the width gains the geometric part
$z_{\mathrm{vis}}\tan\theta$, but $w$ still adds on top, always
\emph{increasing} $|\thdet|$.}
\label{fig:offsetdiagram}
\end{figure}

This single mechanism explains all three observations at once:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{parallel outward ridge}: Eq.~\eqref{eq:offset} is a constant
        displacement $w/z_{\mathrm{vis}}$ in tan-space; converted to degrees
        it is a nearly constant $\sim\arctan(w/z_{\mathrm{vis}})$ shift over
        the populated range;
  \item \textbf{exclusion gap}: no cluster can fit a slope smaller than
        $\sim w/z_{\mathrm{vis}}$, because even a vertical track's cluster is
        $w$ wide over a full-column time span ---
        $|\thdet| \gtrsim \arctan(w/z_{\mathrm{vis}})$ always;
  \item \textbf{horizontal arms}: for $\thref\approx 0$ the geometric width
        vanishes, the sign of the fitted slope is set by noise, and the
        magnitude is again $\sim w/z_{\mathrm{vis}}$ or larger (the events
        that pass the $\geq 4$-strip cut at small angle are precisely those
        with the largest spreading).
\end{itemize}
And because $w$ (a resistive-layer/diffusion property) and $z_{\mathrm{vis}}$
(set by the shared gas supply and thresholds, Sec.~\ref{sec:gap}) are common
to the whole detector family, \textbf{every chamber shows the same
off-diagonal pattern} --- exactly what is observed.

\subsection{Quantitative verification}

All of this is testable in the data of the 2.4\,h run
(\texttt{13\_tpc\_angle\_bias.py}); Figs.~\ref{fig:biasprofiles} and
\ref{fig:clustergeom}:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{the excess is flat in tan-space}: the median of
        $|\tan\thdet| - |\tan\thref|$ is constant at $w/z_{\mathrm{vis}} = 0.115$ for
        $|\thref| \gtrsim \SI{6}{\degree}$, in both planes
        (Fig.~\ref{fig:biasprofiles}, bottom row).
        $\arctan(0.115) = \SI{6.6}{\degree}$ --- the observed ridge
        displacement;
  \item \textbf{wider clusters carry more bias}: splitting by cluster size
        (\numrange{4}{6} / \numrange{7}{9} / $\geq$10 strips) orders the
        excess exactly as the spreading picture predicts;
  \item \textbf{the time span saturates}: the median cluster time span rises
        to a plateau $\Tsat \approx \SI{690}{ns}$ for
        $|\thref| > \SI{10}{\degree}$ --- the full-gap drift time
        (Fig.~\ref{fig:clustergeom}, top row).  (Its decrease towards
        $\theta=0$ is a per-strip integration effect: a vertical track's
        charge lands on few strips whose fitted times average over the
        column, shrinking the strip-to-strip span.);
  \item \textbf{the spatial width has a floor}: the median cluster extent at
        $\thref\approx 0$ is \SIrange{3}{5}{mm} where geometry predicts
        $\approx 0$; at larger angles it grows parallel to the geometric
        expectation $z_{\mathrm{vis}}\tan\theta$, offset by the same floor
        (Fig.~\ref{fig:clustergeom}, bottom row).
\end{itemize}

The ridge-fit intercepts (Sec.~\ref{sec:vdrift}) give the same number from a
completely different projection of the data:
$|b|/v = w/z_{\mathrm{vis}} = 0.115$, i.e.\
$w = \SI{2.2}{mm}$ ($\approx$3 strips) for $z_{\mathrm{vis}}=\SI{19.4}{mm}$.  Finally,
overlaying the parameter-free prediction of Eq.~\eqref{eq:offset} on the
measured density (Fig.~\ref{fig:withprediction}) tracks the ridge in both
planes.

\subsection{Consequences}

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{The production $\sigma$-scan drift velocity is biased.}
        The production estimator (Fig.~\ref{fig:sigscan}) scans $\vd$ and
        minimises the width of $|\thref| - |\thdet|$; the additive offset
        pulls its minimum high ($\vd = 30.5/34.0$~\si{\umns} for X/Y on this run
        versus the unbiased \SI{28.1}{\umns} of Sec.~\ref{sec:vdrift}).
        Its $|\cdot|$-folding also mixes the sign-following offset into the
        width.  Use the ridge fit instead.
  \item \textbf{A first-order angle correction exists}: subtract
        $\mathrm{sign}(\thdet)\, w/z_{\mathrm{vis}}$ in tan-space.  It sharpens inclined
        tracks but cannot recover $|\theta|\lesssim\SI{5}{\degree}$, where the
        angular information is genuinely destroyed by spreading.
  \item \textbf{Angular resolution} (after slicing in $\thref$, i.e.\ with
        the local bias absorbed): $\sigma_\theta \approx
        \SIrange{1.5}{2}{\degree}$ per plane for
        $|\theta|\gtrsim\SI{5}{\degree}$, degrading to $\sim\SI{6}{\degree}$
        near normal incidence (Fig.~\ref{fig:angres}).
\end{enumerate}

%=====================================================================
\section{Drift-velocity measurement from the drift scan}
%=====================================================================
\label{sec:vdrift}

\subsection{A bias-free estimator: the ridge fit}

Rewriting Eq.~\eqref{eq:offset} in terms of the \emph{raw fitted slope}
$S \equiv \vd \tan\thdet$ (in \si{\umns}, after rotation to the M3 frame):
\begin{equation}
  S \;=\; \vd\,\tan\thref \;\pm\; \vd\,\frac{w}{z_{\mathrm{vis}}}.
  \label{eq:ridge}
\end{equation}
This is a straight line in the $(\tan\thref, S)$ plane whose \textbf{slope is
the drift velocity} and whose intercept absorbs the entire spreading offset.
A per-track-sign straight-line fit (iteratively $3\sigma$-clipped, fit window
$0.06<|\tan\thref|<0.55$) therefore measures $\vd$ \emph{without} the bias
that afflicts the $\sigma$-scan --- no assumption about $w$, no folding of
signs, and no drift-velocity prior.  Four independent fits (X/Y plane
$\times$ track sign) are combined by weighted mean; their spread is the
quoted fit systematic.  The M3 angular resolution
($\sim\SI{0.05}{\degree}$) is negligible, so there is no regression dilution.

Two robustness checks: the result is insensitive to the minimum cluster size
(4/5/6 strips give identical $\vd$), and moving the fit window shifts $\vd$ by
up to $\pm$\SI{10}{\percent} (the ridge has a slight curvature --- the
additive model is not perfect at that level).  The default window is anchored
by the independent time-span cross-check below agreeing to \SI{1}{\percent};
we assign $\sim$\SI{5}{\percent} to the absolute scale, while the
\emph{shape} of $\vd$(HV), taken with a fixed window, is much tighter.

\subsection{Cross-check: the visible-column drift time}

The time-span plateau $\Tsat$ (Fig.~\ref{fig:clustergeom}) is an independent
$1/\vd$ observable: $\Tsat = z_{\mathrm{vis}}/\vd$ with the \emph{same}
$z_{\mathrm{vis}}$ at every HV (the visible column is a fixed depth,
Sec.~\ref{sec:gap}).  It shares no systematics with the ridge fit except the
time calibration, but note it is not fully independent as a \emph{velocity}
scale: $z_{\mathrm{vis}}$ itself is calibrated from $\vd\cdot\Tsat$ of the
long run.  What it does test, powerfully, is that
$\vd(\mathrm{HV})\cdot\Tsat(\mathrm{HV})$ is one constant across the scan.
Table~\ref{tab:vdrift} and Fig.~\ref{fig:vdriftscan} show both quantities.

Key points:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Statistics are not a limitation.}  You were right that the
        correlation measures $\vd$ --- and even the 15-minute points give
        \SIrange{1}{3}{\percent} statistical errors, because the ridge fit
        uses every inclined track, not just the width of a projection.
  \item \textbf{Internal consistency} is excellent: $\vd\cdot\Tsat$ is one
        constant wherever the span is not window-limited; the 2.4\,h point
        sits on the scan curve; $w/z_{\mathrm{vis}}$ is field-independent.
  \item \textbf{The low-HV points die by window truncation}, not detector
        effects: at \SI{100}{V} the full-gap drift time
        ($\gtrsim\SI{10}{\micro s}$ for \SI{30}{mm}) vastly exceeds the
        \SI{1.92}{\micro s} DREAM window --- most charge simply arrives after
        the readout closes.  The same truncation (compounded at low field by
        stronger attachment losses, Sec.~\ref{sec:gap}) explains the efficiency
        turn-on of the drift scan (Fig.~\ref{fig:driftscaneff}): \SI{44}{\percent}
        at \SI{100}{V} rising to a \SI{83}{\percent} plateau at
        $\geq\SI{700}{V}$.  This is the quantitative version of the June
        observation ``det3 needs drift $\geq\SI{700}{V}$''.
\end{itemize}

%=====================================================================
\section{The drift gap: \SI{30}{mm} mechanical, \SI{19.4}{mm} visible}
%=====================================================================
\label{sec:gap}

The middle panel of Fig.~\ref{fig:vdriftscan} shows the quantity
$\vd\cdot\Tsat$: it is one constant, $\SI{19.4\pm0.2}{mm}$, across a
$1.6\times$ range of drift field.  A first reading of that number took it to
be the drift gap itself --- \SI{10.6}{mm} short of the \SI{30}{mm} design.
The mechanical gap, however, is known to be close to \SI{30}{mm}
(certainly $>\SI{27}{mm}$), and a dedicated test
(\texttt{17\_gap\_attachment\_test.py}, Fig.~\ref{fig:gaptest}) resolves the
conflict cleanly: \textbf{the gap is \SI{30}{mm}; \SI{19.4}{mm} is the depth
of drift column whose signal survives above threshold}.  Electrons produced
higher up \emph{do} arrive --- with exponentially attenuated amplitude ---
because they are progressively captured (attachment) in the contaminated gas
of Sec.~\ref{sec:gas}.

\subsection{What $\vd\cdot\Tsat$ actually measures}

$\Tsat$ is the plateau of the \emph{cluster} time span, and a cluster only
contains strips above threshold.  The product $\vd\cdot\Tsat$ is therefore
the depth of the \emph{visible} column, $z_{\mathrm{vis}}$: the height at
which the per-strip amplitude falls below the hit threshold.  Its constancy
with drift field says that this threshold contour sits at a fixed
\emph{depth} --- which is exactly what a depth-dependent survival probability
(attachment over a fixed length scale) produces.  Two properties of the
product remain valuable: it is immune to time-calibration errors (velocity
$\propto 1/k$, span $\propto k$ under any time rescaling $k$), and it is the
correct normalisation for the micro-TPC geometry of
Secs.~\ref{sec:offdiag}--\ref{sec:vdrift}, which live entirely inside the
visible column.

\subsection{The discriminating test}

If the column geometrically ended at \SI{19.4}{mm} there would be
\emph{nothing but noise floor} beyond $\Tsat$ in the strip arrival-time
distribution.  If instead the gap is \SI{30}{mm} with progressive charge
loss, the distribution must carry a suppressed tail that terminates at the
\SI{30}{mm} drift time, and the strip \emph{amplitude} must decay with drift
depth.  Figure~\ref{fig:gaptest} tests this per drift-scan point, with all
times converted to depth via $z=\vd\,t$:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{the tails do not end at \SI{19.4}{mm}} (top left): every HV
        point shows the same shape --- flat plateau to \SI{19.4}{mm}, then an
        exponential shoulder falling $\sim$2 orders of magnitude and
        terminating at \SIrange{28}{29}{mm} where the readout window permits
        (\SIrange{900}{1100}{V}; the \SI{700}{V} endpoint at \SI{25.7}{mm}
        and the \SI{500}{V} one at \SI{20}{mm} are window-truncated lower
        bounds).  The tail endpoint sits at a fixed \emph{depth}, i.e.\ it
        scales as $1/\vd$ in time (bottom row) --- a geometric edge at
        $\approx\SI{30}{mm}$, softened by the threshold;
  \item \textbf{the amplitude decays with depth, identically at every field}
        (Fig.~\ref{fig:ampattach} and top right of
        Fig.~\ref{fig:gaptest}): versus drift \emph{time} the decay
        timescale changes with HV, but versus drift \emph{distance} the
        \SIrange{700}{1100}{V} curves collapse onto one exponential ---
        $\lambda \approx \SI{10}{mm}$ fitted over \SIrange{8}{24}{mm},
        \SIrange{13}{15}{mm} inside the visible column --- declining by an
        order of magnitude between mid-column and \SI{30}{mm}.  Signal loss
        is a function of \emph{how far the charge drifts}, not of time or
        field --- the fingerprint of capture on electronegative impurities
        (O$_2$), with transverse diffusion contributing a milder per-strip
        dilution on top;
  \item \textbf{the ``cliff'' is a threshold crossing}: the arrival-time
        distribution stays flat while the amplitude distribution clears the
        hit threshold, then falls over a few bins as the median approaches
        it --- steeper than the amplitude decay itself because the occupancy
        integrates the amplitude distribution above a cut.  The earlier
        reading of this edge as a hard geometric boundary
        (Fig.~\ref{fig:ampvstime}) missed that the exponential tail beyond it
        ends precisely at the \SI{30}{mm} drift time (dotted line in the
        right panel).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Consistency of the other observables}

The single-strip pulse widths (Fig.~\ref{fig:pulsewidth}) measure the
duration over which a vertical track's lead strip stays above threshold ---
i.e.\ the \emph{surviving} column's drift time.  Their $\sim\SI{700}{ns}$
scale is consistent with the attachment picture and carries no independent
information about the mechanical gap; it is no longer evidence for a
\SI{19.4}{mm} electrode spacing.

\subsection{Interpretation and consequences}

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Field scale}: with $g=\SI{30}{mm}$ the drift field is
        $E = V_{\mathrm{drift}}/\SI{3.0}{cm}$ (\SI{1000}{V} $\to$
        \SI{333}{\volt\per\centi\meter}); Sec.~\ref{sec:gas} uses this
        throughout.  The ridge-fit velocities are unaffected --- the fitted
        slope is a local $\mathrm{d}x/\mathrm{d}t$ inside the visible column.
  \item \textbf{$z_{\mathrm{vis}}$ is a gas-quality number, not a
        detector constant}: it is where the survival probability
        $\exp(-z/\lambda_{\mathrm{att}})$ meets the threshold.  Cleaning the
        gas should push the visible column towards the full \SI{30}{mm} ---
        lengthening $\Tsat$ (mind the \SI{1.92}{\micro s} window:
        \SI{30}{mm} at \SI{28}{\umns} is \SI{1067}{ns}, still comfortable),
        increasing the collected charge, and shrinking the off-diagonal
        offset $w/z_{\mathrm{vis}}$ of Sec.~\ref{sec:offdiag}.
  \item \textbf{Charge budget}: roughly a third of each track's ionisation is
        currently lost in transit, and what survives from mid-column is
        attenuated --- a direct efficiency and signal-to-noise cost on top of
        the drift-speed problem.
  \item The \SI{60}{ns} DREAM sampling period assumption still deserves a
        one-line confirmation; it rescales $\vd$ and all times together but
        no conclusion here depends on it.
\end{itemize}

\section{Gas diagnosis: air/moisture contamination of the Ar/iso 95/5}
%=====================================================================
\label{sec:gas}

\subsection{The discrepancy}

With the \SI{30}{mm} gap of Sec.~\ref{sec:gap} the field scale is
$E = V_{\mathrm{drift}}/\SI{3.0}{cm}$ (\SI{1000}{V} $\to$
\SI{333}{\volt\per\centi\meter}), and the measured $\vd(E)$ can be confronted
with Magboltz (Garfield++ \texttt{MediumMagboltz}, Saclay pressure
\SI{745.8}{Torr}, \SI{293}{K}).  For \emph{clean} Ar/iC$_4$H$_{10}$ 95/5 the
prediction peaks at \SI{41}{\umns} near \SI{210}{\volt\per\centi\meter} and
\emph{decreases} slowly above; the measurement instead rises monotonically
over the whole scan, is a factor $\sim$3 low at
\SI{167}{\volt\per\centi\meter} (\SI{12.8} vs $\sim$\SI{40}{\umns}), and
reaches only \SI{28}{\umns} at the operating field where clean gas predicts
\SI{39}{\umns}.  The shape disagreement is the crucial part: no rescaling of
times, fields or gap can turn a curve that peaks at
\SI{210}{\volt\per\centi\meter} into one still rising at
\SI{370}{\volt\per\centi\meter}.  This is a \emph{gas property}.

\subsection{Candidate hypotheses: velocity alone is ambiguous}

{\sloppy Magboltz was run for a set of candidate gases over the drift-field
range (scripts \texttt{mm\_drift\_velocity\_candidates.py},
\texttt{mm\_attachment\_\{table,air\_candidates\}.py} in
\texttt{garfield\_sim/}); Fig.~\ref{fig:magboltz} and
Table~\ref{tab:candidates}:\par}

The velocity curve leaves \emph{two} viable candidates --- a wrong bottle of
Ar/CO$_2$ 90/10, or water contamination at the
\SIrange{1}{1.5}{\percent} level --- and they are separated by the two other
observables in hand:

\paragraph{Amplification.}  A Magboltz Townsend-coefficient comparison over
the \SI{150}{\micro\meter} amplification gap
(\texttt{mm\_townsend\_compare.py}) puts every iso-gain point of
Ar/CO$_2$ 90/10 about \SI{50}{V} \emph{above} Ar/iso 95/5 (gain $10^2$:
\SI{528}{V} vs \SI{475}{V}; gain $10^3$: \SI{631}{V} vs \SI{587}{V}).  The
observed turn-on at \SI{425}{V} with full plateau by \SI{455}{V}
(Sec.~\ref{sec:hvscans}) matches the Ar/iso expectation; with Ar/CO$_2$ the
whole efficiency curve would sit $\sim$\SI{50}{V} higher.  Percent-level
H$_2$O/air, by contrast, leaves the gain curve essentially untouched.

\paragraph{Attachment.}  Clean Ar/CO$_2$ does not attach electrons at drift
fields ($\eta = 0$ in Magboltz) --- it cannot produce the amplitude decay of
Fig.~\ref{fig:gaptest}.  Neither, notably, can water alone: Magboltz gives
$\eta = 0$ for all Ar/iso + H$_2$O mixtures at these fields (water's
dissociative-attachment threshold, $\sim$\SI{5}{eV}, is far above drift
energies).  The measured decay needs \textbf{oxygen}.

\subsection{The attachment cross-check closes on O$_2$}

Figure~\ref{fig:attclose} compares the measured amplitude-decay length from
the drift scan with the Magboltz attachment length $1/\eta(E)$ for
air-doped mixtures.  The measured mid-column
$\lambda \approx \SIrange{13}{15}{mm}$ at \SIrange{230}{370}{\volt\per\centi\meter}
falls between the \SI{1}{\percent}-air (\SI{0.21}{\percent} O$_2$,
$\lambda\approx\SIrange{16}{21}{mm}$) and \SI{2}{\percent}-air
(\SI{0.42}{\percent} O$_2$, $\lambda\approx\SI{10}{mm}$) predictions:
\textbf{an O$_2$ fraction of $\sim$\SIrange{0.2}{0.4}{\percent} reproduces
the observed signal loss}.  (The measured $\lambda$ also absorbs some
per-strip dilution from transverse diffusion, so the true attachment length
is slightly longer and the implied O$_2$ correspondingly at the lower end.)

\centering

\caption{Measured signal-decay length vs drift field (black: mid-column;
grey: beyond $\Tsat$, steepened by the threshold), compared with Magboltz

O$_2$ (clean Ar/CO$_2$, Ar/iso + H$_2$O only) predict no attachment at all.}

\end{figure}

\subsection{Interpretation: one contamination, both anomalies}

Everything points at a single cause --- \textbf{air and moisture in the
drift gas} at the percent level:
\begin{itemize}
  \item $\sim$\SIrange{1}{1.5}{\percent} H$_2$O reproduces the slow,
        monotonically rising $\vd(E)$ (water's huge dipole cross-section
        thermalises the electrons at low field).  That is
        $\sim$\SIrange{40}{70}{\percent} relative humidity worth of water ---
        ordinary damp-air/outgassing levels, not a flooded line;
  \item $\sim$\SIrange{0.2}{0.4}{\percent} O$_2$ (i.e.\
        $\sim$\SIrange{1}{2}{\percent} air-equivalent) reproduces the
        attachment that truncates the visible drift column at
        \SI{19.4}{mm} (Sec.~\ref{sec:gap});
  \item the amplification is insensitive to impurities at this level ---
        consistent with the resist-HV scans matching clean-gas gain;
  \item water in excess of the O$_2$-implied air fraction is expected:
        H$_2$O permeates plastics and outgasses from glued structures far
        faster than O$_2$ enters, the classic signature of an unflushed or
        under-flushed detector volume.
\end{itemize}

Likely entry points: an unflushed detector volume, permeable or long tubing,
very low flow rate, or a leaky fitting.  Decisive follow-ups:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{fresh-gas re-scan} of one detector after a long flush: at
        \SI{1000}{V} the velocity should rise from 28 towards
        $\sim$\SI{39}{\umns}, \emph{and} the visible column should extend
        from \SI{19.4}{mm} towards the full \SI{30}{mm} (with the amplitude
        decay flattening) --- a one-hour measurement with the ridge fit plus
        the Fig.~\ref{fig:gaptest} analysis;
  \item humidity \emph{and} O$_2$ probes on the gas exhaust; a flow/leak
        check of the line.
\end{itemize}

Beyond drift speed, the contamination costs signal: a third of each track's
charge is captured before reaching the mesh, and the enhanced transverse
diffusion feeds the charge-spreading width $w$ of Sec.~\ref{sec:offdiag}.
Cleaning the gas should improve efficiency, signal-to-noise, micro-TPC angle
quality, and the small-angle exclusion gap simultaneously.

\section{Summary and follow-ups}
%=====================================================================

\subsection{Summary}

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Off-diagonal angle correlation --- solved.}  It is the
        additive charge-spreading bias
        $\tan\thdet = \tan\thref \pm w/z_{\mathrm{vis}}$,
        $w/z_{\mathrm{vis}} = 0.115$
        ($w\approx\SI{2.2}{mm}$): a detector-family constant.  Not a
        rotation; not a bug.  It also explains the
        $|\thdet|\gtrsim\SI{5}{\degree}$ exclusion gap and the horizontal
        arms at $\thref\approx0$.
  \item \textbf{Drift velocity --- measured.}  The ridge fit turns the angle
        correlation into a precision $\vd$ measurement:
        $\vd(\SI{1000}{V}) = \SI{28.1\pm0.7}{\umns}$, full curve in
        Table~\ref{tab:vdrift}, \SIrange{1}{3}{\percent} per 15-minute point.
  \item \textbf{Drift gap --- \SI{30}{mm} mechanical, \SI{19.4}{mm}
        visible.}  The clock-immune product $\vd\Tsat = \SI{19.4\pm0.2}{mm}$
        is the depth at which drifting charge falls below threshold, not the
        electrode spacing: the arrival-time tail continues (exponentially
        suppressed) out to the \SI{30}{mm} drift time at every field, and
        the strip amplitude decays with drift \emph{depth}
        ($\lambda\approx\SIrange{13}{15}{mm}$, one universal curve for all
        fields) --- charge from the far third of the gap is lost to
        attachment in the contaminated gas.
  \item \textbf{Gas --- contaminated with an electronegative polar impurity.}
        With the \SI{30}{mm} field scale ($E=V/\SI{3.0}{cm}$) the measured
        $\vd(E)$ and the attachment losses point together at air/moisture
        ingress into the Ar/iso 95/5 (Sec.~\ref{sec:gas}); clean nominal gas
        and iso-rich mixing errors are excluded, and the gain behaviour rules
        out a wrong-bottle quencher swap.
  \item \textbf{Operating point.}  det3: resist \SIrange{470}{480}{V}
        (same efficiency as \SI{490}{V}, half the sparks), drift
        $\geq\SI{700}{V}$ (readout-window constraint), \SI{80}{\percent}
        reconstruction efficiency, \SI{0.83}{}/\SI{0.92}{mm} core
        resolution, \SIrange{1.5}{2}{\degree} micro-TPC angular resolution
        for inclined tracks.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Follow-ups}

\begin{enumerate}
  \item The Saturday long run is \emph{complete} at \SI{141}{min} (it was
        stopped early when the p2 overnight run started at 01:33) --- for
        more det3 statistics use the p2 run (53k rays) or take a new
        dedicated long run; the \emph{reco-far} tail difference vs the p2
        run can be probed with the existing data.
  \item Gas system: fresh-gas re-scan after a long flush (one hour decides
        the contamination hypothesis --- the velocity at fixed HV should
        jump \emph{and} the visible column should extend towards
        \SI{30}{mm}); humidity/O$_2$ probe on the exhaust; review flush time,
        flow rate and tubing permeability.
  \item Mechanics: confirm the \SI{30}{mm} drift-gap drawing (the tail
        endpoint gives \SIrange{28}{29}{mm} from data); confirm the DREAM
        sampling period (\SI{60}{ns}) to fix the absolute time scale.
  \item Analysis: wire the ridge-fit $\vd$ and the $w/g$ angle correction
        into the production pipeline (\texttt{03\_alignment\_and\_tpc.py});
        revisit micro-TPC angular resolution after gas drying.
  \item Longer readout window (or slower drift-field points avoided) if
        low-field drift data are wanted in the future.
\end{enumerate}
